I could write (and likely will) write an essay on what I thought made this team AI/ML team so great to work with, and tackle the intricate problem of developing reliable agents for other enterprises. But now I actually want to write about the other significant bit contributing to shipping at Zendesk - product management. Before Zendesk, I thought this was a buzzword and I didn't have a datapoint on what this actually meant. I had worked in research labs, and research forward companies, but without being as close to the product as I was at Zendesk, I didn't actually know what I was missing. In my naive view, companies had R&D departments which worked on research and developed new features that would be useful to customers. Little did I realize that there is a ton of work that needs to go into defining what features need to be built, what goals are realistic, how to manage an prioritize resources in a team, all the while needing to balance current customer bugs with development of new features. Countless times over the summer, I saw how complex this system was. Nearly every decision being made by the incredible PM I worked for was a strategic judgment call. Do we spend time developing new agentic features for the product or devote engineering time to refactoring our internal libraries? Will increasing the prioritization of testing and reliability of our product actually help our product development cycle or merely hamper our velocity. Is there a way that we can do so without spurring the latter? Can we start staffing one or two people solely to focus on long-tail research without a guarantee of directly helping features for several months? How do we parallelize work within our team, but also make sure that our services are available for use and documented for other teams in the organization? These are just some of the questions that I know my PM was considering. Of course, there are likely hundreds more that I can't even fathom, but I think this illustrates how intricate, but also interdisciplinary the domain can be. In fact, I think one of the key features of all of these questions is that there is not some a set framework for going about answering them. It takes years of experience, judgment, trial-and-error, and iteration to understand what would work best in a given scenario. Coming from a research background where we also tackle open-ended questions, I see some similarities, but within completely different domains. In product management, the opened-question seems to be the 'what' versus within research and core software development, the open-ended questions relate to the 'how.'
